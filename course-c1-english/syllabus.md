# Official Syllabus — English C1 Advanced

> **Framework:** Common European Framework of Reference for Languages (CEFR) — Level C1  
> **Total units:** 60 · **Modules:** 10 · **Lessons per unit:** 6  
> **Estimated hours:** 300–360 hours of instruction  

---

## CEFR C1 Can-Do Statements

### Reading
- Can understand long, complex factual and literary texts, appreciating distinctions of style
- Can understand specialised articles and longer technical instructions, even when they do not relate to his/her field
- Can identify main conclusions in argumentative texts and recognise the line of argument
- Can understand implicit attitudes, opinions and irony in complex texts

### Listening
- Can understand extended speech even when it is not clearly structured and when relationships are only implied
- Can understand television programmes, films and broadcasts with ease, including abstract and complex topics
- Can follow extended speech and complex lines of argument, provided the topic is reasonably familiar
- Can understand a wide range of idiomatic expressions and colloquialisms, appreciating shifts in register

### Writing
- Can write clear, well-structured expositions of complex subjects, underlining relevant salient issues
- Can write different kinds of texts in a consistent, appropriate style with accurate grammar
- Can write summaries of professional or literary texts, building on key points and arguments
- Can produce a well-argued essay presenting a case, giving reasons in support of or against a particular point of view

### Speaking
- Can express him/herself fluently and spontaneously, almost effortlessly
- Can use language flexibly and effectively for social, academic and professional purposes
- Can formulate ideas and opinions with precision and relate his/her contribution skillfully to those of other speakers
- Can give clear, well-structured presentations on complex subjects

### Grammar
- Maintains consistent grammatical control of complex language, even while attention is otherwise engaged
- Uses language with flexibility and precision
- Controls a large repertoire of complex grammatical structures with infrequent slips or non-systematic errors

## Grammar Scope and Sequence

### Module 1 — Identity and Society (Units 1–6)

| Unit | Grammar Structure | Form | Key Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Modal verbs — deduction & probability | *must/can't/could/might/should have + past participle* | *She must have known. He can't have done it.* |
| 2 | Perfect aspect nuances | *Present perfect simple vs continuous; perfect infinitive* | *I've been working here for years. He seems to have left.* |
| 3 | Advanced reported speech | Complex transformations; reporting verbs with variations | *He alleged that… She denied having…* |
| 4 | Advanced passive voice | *Have sth done; get sth done; passive with modals* | *The report is believed to be… I had my car fixed.* |
| 5 | Cleft sentences | *It is/was… that/who…; What + clause + is/was…* | *It was the cost that concerned us. What I find remarkable is…* |
| 6 | Module 1 consolidation | Review of all Module 1 structures | Mixed practice |

### Module 2 — Language and Communication (Units 7–12)

| Unit | Grammar Structure | Form | Key Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| 7 | Inversion for emphasis | *Never/Rarely/Seldom/Not only + auxiliary + subject* | *Never have I seen such dedication. Not only did she pass…* |
| 8 | Complex noun phrases | Pre- and post-modification; nominalisations | *The much-debated proposal to reduce… / The fact that…* |
| 9 | Advanced discourse markers | Organising, contrasting, conceding, adding | *Notwithstanding this, … / With regard to… / In so far as…* |
| 10 | Subjunctive | *It is essential/vital/necessary that + bare infinitive* | *I suggest that he attend. It is imperative that she be informed.* |
| 11 | Ellipsis and substitution | Omitting repeated elements; *do so, one, ones, such* | *She loves opera and so does he. I've read the long one but not the short one.* |
| 12 | Module 2 consolidation | Review of all Module 2 structures | Mixed practice |

### Module 3 — Science and Technology (Units 13–18)

| Unit | Grammar Structure | Form | Key Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| 13 | Mixed conditionals | *If + past perfect… would/might + bare infinitive (present result)* | *If I had studied medicine, I would be a doctor now.* |
| 14 | Conditional inversion | *Had I…, Were it not for…, Should you need…* | *Had I known, I would have acted differently.* |
| 15 | Advanced future forms | *be about to / be due to / be bound to / be set to / be likely to* | *The project is due to launch next month.* |
| 16 | Participle clauses | *Having + past participle; -ing / -ed clauses as adverbials* | *Having considered all options, we decided to… / Faced with rising costs…* |
| 17 | Wish / If only | *Wish + past simple/continuous/perfect; If only…* | *I wish I had taken that job. If only she had been there.* |
| 18 | Module 3 consolidation | Review of all Module 3 structures | Mixed practice |

### Module 4 — The Natural World (Units 19–24)

| Unit | Grammar Structure | Form | Key Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| 19 | Advanced relative clauses | Non-defining with prepositions; reduced relatives; *whereby, wherein* | *The policy, which led to… / The scientist working on this…* |
| 20 | Absolute constructions | Nominative absolutes; *weather permitting; all things considered* | *The conference over, delegates left. All being well, we'll finish on time.* |
| 21 | Emphasis — auxiliary do | *Do/does/did* for emphasis; fronting for focus | *I do believe this is crucial. Never before has such a…* |
| 22 | Hedging language | *tend to, appear to, seem to, it could be argued, arguably* | *It would appear that… / This arguably suggests…* |
| 23 | Comment clauses | *which is…, as I see it, it must be said, what's more* | *The results, which is surprising, showed… / It has to be said that…* |
| 24 | Module 4 consolidation | Review of all Module 4 structures | Mixed practice |

## Vocabulary Progression Framework

### Integration Approach

| Phase | Modules | Vocabulary Focus |
|---|---|---|
| Activation (B2→C1) | M1–M3 | Thematic expansion, collocation building, register awareness |
| Consolidation | M4–M6 | Academic Word List integration, phrasal verbs, formal synonyms |
| Refinement | M7–M9 | Precision, nuance, hedging vocabulary, technical registers |
| Synthesis | M10 | Abstract vocabulary, philosophical lexis, full AWL consolidation |

### Vocabulary Components per Unit

- **~30–40 thematic lexical items** — core words and phrases in context
- **5+ collocations** — high-frequency noun/verb/adjective combinations
- **3–5 phrasal verbs** — advanced, contextualised
- **3–5 idioms or fixed expressions** — where thematically relevant
- **Word formation patterns** — introduced in Units 2, 11, 16, 29, 39, 57

## Assessment Structure

### Weighting

| Component | Weight | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Writing Portfolio | 25% | Best 10 writing tasks across the course |
| Listening Tests | 15% | One test per module (10 tests) |
| Reading Tests | 15% | One test per module (10 tests) |
| Speaking Assessments | 25% | One assessment per module (10 assessments) |
| Grammar & Vocabulary Tests | 20% | One test per module (10 tests) |

### Module Assessment (every 6 units)

Each module ends with a **Review Unit** (units 6, 12, 18, 24, 30, 36, 42, 48, 54, 60) containing:
- Integrated reading and listening comprehension test
- Grammar and vocabulary test covering all module structures
- Extended writing task (400–500 words)
- Spoken performance assessment (individual + interactive)

## Pedagogical Sequence (per unit)

Each of the 60 units follows this 6-lesson sequence:

1. **L1 — Reading** — Authentic or adapted text (400–700 words at C1 level) with comprehension, inference and vocabulary tasks
2. **L2 — Listening** — Audio input 3–5 minutes with global, specific and inferential comprehension questions
3. **L3 — Grammar** — Presentation of target structure (form + use), controlled practice, then communicative application
4. **L4 — Vocabulary** — Thematic lexis presented in context, collocations work, gap-fill and production exercises
5. **L5 — Writing** — Analysis of a model text, guided writing task (250–400 words), peer/self-assessment criteria
6. **L6 — Speaking + Review** — Structured oral task (debate/presentation/role-play), unit can-do checklist review

## How to Read a Unit File

Each unit file (`units/unit-XX-theme.md`) covers one full **module** (6 units × 6 lessons) and is organised as:

1. **Module overview** — theme, grammar focus, vocabulary areas, text types
2. **Unit-by-unit breakdown** — for each of the 6 units:
   - Learning objectives (CEFR can-do statements)
   - L1–L6 lesson descriptions with activities and tasks
   - Grammar focus: form, use, examples
   - Vocabulary list (~30–40 items with collocations)
   - Writing task with criteria
   - Speaking task with assessment focus
   - Unit evaluation checklist
