### L3 — Grammar: Mixed Conditionals

**Target structures:** Type 3 condition + Type 2 result · Type 2 condition + Type 3 result

**Form:**

| Type | Condition clause | Result clause | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| **3→2** (past → present) | *If + past perfect* | *would/might/could + bare infinitive* | Unreal past situation with present result |
| **2→3** (present → past) | *If + past simple* | *would/might/could + have + past participle* | Unreal present situation with past result |

**Examples:**

| Mixed conditional | Meaning |
|---|---|
| *If they had invested in AI earlier, they would be the market leader now.* | They didn't invest → they are not the leader |
| *If she weren't afraid of technology, she would have applied for the job.* | She is afraid → she didn't apply |
| *If AI had never been developed, our world might look very different today.* | AI was developed → world is as it is |

**Use notes:**
- Mixed conditionals express **cause-and-effect relationships across time**
- *Might* and *could* replace *would* for less certainty
- Negative mixed conditionals are also common: *If it hadn't been for AI, we wouldn't have…*

**Exercises:**
1. Complete 8 mixed conditional sentences with the correct form
2. Identify whether each conditional is 3→2 or 2→3 and explain the time relationship
3. Write 4 original mixed conditional sentences about the topic of AI and its impact

### L5 — Writing: Formal Proposal

**Task:** Write a formal proposal (240–280 words):  
*You work for a city council. Write a proposal recommending the introduction of an AI-powered system to improve public transport efficiency.*

**Proposal structure:**
```
Title: Proposal for the Introduction of…
Introduction: Purpose and background
Proposed Solution: What is being suggested and why
Benefits: Expected positive outcomes
Potential Concerns and Mitigations: Anticipated objections + responses
Conclusion: Summary + recommendation
```

**Language of proposals:**

- *It is proposed that… / We recommend the introduction of…*

### L3 — Grammar: Conditional Inversion

**Target structures:** *Had I…, Were it not for…, Should you…*

**Form:**

| Original conditional | Inverted form | Register |
|---|---|---|
| *If I had known, I would have acted.* | *Had I known, I would have acted.* | Formal/literary |
| *If it were not for the risks…* | *Were it not for the risks…* | Formal |
| *If this were to happen…* | *Were this to happen…* | Formal/academic |
| *If you should need assistance…* | *Should you need assistance…* | Formal/polite |
| *If I had been more cautious…* | *Had I been more cautious…* | Formal/literary |

**Use notes:**
- Conditional inversion is formed by moving the auxiliary to the front and **removing** *if*
- Common in **formal writing, academic texts, legal language and speeches**
- *Had* (past perfect) = 3rd conditional · *Were* = 2nd conditional formal · *Should* = possibility

**Exercises:**
1. Transform 8 *if*-conditionals into inverted form
2. Expand 4 inverted conditionals back into full *if* form and state the type
3. Write a formal 80-word paragraph on a bioethical topic using at least 2 inverted conditionals

### L5 — Writing: Discursive Essay (Double Perspective)

**Task:** Write a discursive essay (400–460 words):  
*"The ability to edit the human genome offers more benefits than risks." Discuss.*

**Essay structure:**
1. **Introduction** — Introduce topic; state essay will consider both sides
2. **Body 1** — Arguments in favour (gene editing for disease)
3. **Body 2** — Arguments against (ethics, inequality, unknown risks)
4. **Conclusion** — Synthesise; give a qualified conclusion

**Language of balance:**
- *Proponents argue that… / Critics contend that…*
- *On the one hand… / On the other hand…*
- *While there are clear benefits… / It cannot be denied that the risks…*
- *Ultimately, it seems that… / It would appear that the answer lies somewhere between…*

### L3 — Grammar: Advanced Future Forms

**Target structures:** *be about to / be due to / be bound to / be set to / be likely to / be on the verge of*

**Form and meaning:**

| Expression | Form | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| *be about to* | *am/is/are about to + infinitive* | Imminent future | *The rocket is about to launch.* |
| *be due to* | *am/is/are due to + infinitive* | Scheduled/expected | *The mission is due to land in 2035.* |
| *be bound to* | *am/is/are bound to + infinitive* | Certainty (inevitable) | *This discovery is bound to change everything.* |
| *be set to* | *am/is/are set to + infinitive* | Planned, on course | *The company is set to announce its Mars plans.* |
| *be likely to* | *am/is/are likely to + infinitive* | Probability | *Costs are likely to decrease over time.* |
| *be on the verge of* | *am/is/are on the verge of + -ing* | Very soon / about to | *We are on the verge of making a breakthrough.* |

**Contrasts:**
- *be about to* = seconds/minutes away (imminent)
- *be due to* = scheduled according to a plan
- *be bound to* = certainty, not controllable
- *be set to* = deliberate plan, positive framing
- *be likely to* = probability (~70%+)

### L3 — Grammar: Participle Clauses

**Target structures:** *-ing* participle clauses · *-ed* participle clauses · Perfect participle clauses

**Form:**

| Type | Form | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Present participle | *-ing + rest of clause* | Simultaneous action / cause | *Working from home, many employees reported higher productivity.* |
| Past participle | *-ed + rest of clause* | Passive meaning / result | *Founded in a garage, the company grew into a global giant.* |
| Perfect participle | *Having + past participle* | Earlier past action | *Having considered all the evidence, the board decided to invest.* |
| Negative participle | *Not + -ing/-ed* | Negated simultaneous/causal | *Not knowing the risks, they proceeded with the plan.* |

**Use notes:**
- The subject of the participle clause **must be the same as the main clause subject**
- Participle clauses are common in **formal writing and literary English**
- They add information concisely, avoiding relative clauses or full subordinate clauses

**Exercises:**
1. Combine 8 pairs of sentences into one using a participle clause
2. Identify and classify 6 participle clauses in a text

### L3 — Grammar: Wish / If only / It's time / I'd rather

**Target structures:** Expressing regret, preference and desire about past, present and future

**Form:**

| Expression | Time reference | Form | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| *wish + past simple* | Present | Desire for current situation to be different | *I wish technology were more equitable.* |

| *wish + past perfect* | Past | Regret about past situation | *I wish they had consulted ethicists earlier.* |

| *If only* | All | Stronger form of wish | *If only we had acted sooner on climate change.* |
| *It's (high/about) time* | Present/Future | Something should happen | *It's high time we took data privacy seriously.* |
| *I'd rather + past simple* | Present/Future preference | | *I'd rather they introduced the technology gradually.* |
| *I'd rather + have + pp* | Past preference | | *I'd rather we had waited for more testing.* |

**Exercises:**
