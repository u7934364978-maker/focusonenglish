## L1 — Reading

### Text

**Type:** Academic article (adapted, 500–600 words)  
**Title:** *Who Are We? Self-Concept, Social Perception and Identity*  
**Source style:** Psychology Today / The Guardian Long Read

---

*We like to think of ourselves as fixed, knowable entities — a stable core of personality that persists regardless of circumstance. Yet psychologists have long argued that the self is far more fluid and context-dependent than this comfortable illusion suggests. Our self-concept, the mental image we hold of ourselves, is not simply discovered — it is actively constructed, shaped by the people around us, the cultural narratives we inhabit, and the roles we perform in daily life.*

*The sociologist Erving Goffman famously proposed that social life is a kind of performance — that we present different versions of ourselves depending on the audience. This is not dishonesty, he argued, but a sophisticated and largely unconscious process of social calibration. The version of yourself that walks into a job interview is not identical to the self that laughs with close friends; yet both are genuinely you.*

*What, then, constitutes authentic identity? Many argue that authenticity lies not in consistency across all contexts, but in the degree to which one's actions align with one's deeply held values. A person who knows their own principles and acts accordingly — even when it is difficult — demonstrates what psychologists term a high degree of self-concept clarity. Interestingly, research suggests that this clarity is strongly associated with psychological resilience: the capacity to recover from adversity without losing one's sense of self.*

*The digital age has introduced a new and troubling dimension to this picture. Social media platforms encourage users to curate an idealised version of the self — carefully filtered and strategically shared. Critics worry that this curated self, shaped partly by algorithmic feedback loops, is gradually replacing the messy, multifaceted reality of who we actually are. Others, however, argue that self-presentation online is simply Goffman's performance theory applied to a new stage.*

*Perhaps the most honest answer is that identity has never been a single, unchanging thing. It is a process — nuanced, ongoing, and irreducibly social. To ask "who are you, really?" may be to ask the wrong question. The more productive inquiry might be: who are you becoming, and in whose company?*

## L3 — Grammar: Modal Verbs — Deduction and Probability

### Target Structures

*must, can't, could, might, may, should have + past participle*

---

### Form

| Degree of certainty | Present / Future | Past |
|---|---|---|
| **Certain (positive)** | *She must be tired.* | *She must have been tired.* |
| **Certain (negative)** | *He can't be serious.* | *He can't have said that.* |
| **Possible** | *It could / might / may be true.* | *It could / might have happened.* |
| **Expected (but uncertain)** | *She should be here by now.* | *He should have arrived already.* |

---

### Use Notes

- **Must** → strong logical deduction (positive). NOT obligation in this context.
  - *She's been studying for 10 hours — she must be exhausted.*
- **Can't** → strong logical deduction (negative). Equivalent to "it's impossible that".
  - *He can't have known — he was in another country.*
- **Could / might / may** → possibility. *Might* is slightly more tentative than *could* or *may*.
  - *She might be right. / That could explain it. / It may have been a mistake.*
- **Should have** → something expected but possibly not fulfilled.
  - *They should have received the email by now.*
- **Must have / can't have** → deductions about the past (very common at C1).
  - *The lights are off — they must have left already.*

---

### Common Errors at B2→C1

| Error | Correction | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| *He must have been lie.* | *He must have been lying.* | Use continuous infinitive for ongoing action |
| *She can't be do that.* | *She can't be doing that.* / *She can't have done that.* | Modal + be + -ing OR modal + have + pp |
| *It might has happened.* | *It might have happened.* | After modal, always use bare infinitive: *have* (not *has*) |
| *He should arrived.* | *He should have arrived.* | Must use *have* + past participle for past reference |

## L5 — Writing: Argumentative Essay — Opening Paragraph

### The Task

> **Essay title:**  
> *"Our cultural background is the most important factor in shaping who we are." Discuss.*

**Writing focus:** Thesis statement · Signposting · Academic register · Hedging language

---

### What Makes a Strong C1 Opening Paragraph?

A C1 argumentative essay opening contains:

| Element | Purpose | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Hook** | Engages the reader; establishes the topic | *Identity is one of the most complex and debated concepts in contemporary psychology.* |
| **Background statement** | Contextualises the argument | *Cultural background undeniably plays a central role in shaping values, beliefs and behaviours.* |
| **Concession** | Acknowledges the opposing view | *While it is true that culture exerts a powerful influence…* |
| **Thesis** | States the writer's position clearly | *…this essay will argue that identity is shaped by an intricate interplay of factors.* |
| **Signpost** | Indicates the essay structure | *The following paragraphs will examine culture, personal experience and innate characteristics in turn.* |

---

### Model Paragraph

> *Identity is one of the most complex and debated concepts in contemporary psychology. While it is undeniable that cultural background plays a significant role in shaping an individual's values, beliefs and behaviours, it would be an oversimplification to claim it is the single most important factor. This essay will argue that identity is the product of an intricate interplay between culture, personal experience and innate characteristics. The following paragraphs will assess the relative weight of each, drawing on evidence from psychology and sociology.*

---

### Analysis Tasks

1. Identify the **hook**, **background statement**, **thesis** and **signpost sentence** in the model.
2. Underline all examples of **hedging language** (*it would be, undeniable, significant, intricate*).
3. What verb phrase does the writer use to announce the essay's argument? Could you use an alternative?
4. What register features mark this paragraph as **formal and academic**?

---

### Production Task

> Write your own opening paragraph (~100 words) for **one** of the following titles:

- *"Social media has made it harder to develop an authentic identity."*
- *"We are who we choose to be, not who we are born to be."*
- *"Modern society places too much emphasis on individual identity."*

**Assessment criteria:**
- [ ] Clear thesis that responds directly to the question
- [ ] Academic register throughout
- [ ] Hedging language used appropriately
- [ ] Logical structure: hook → context → concession → thesis → signpost
- [ ] Appropriate length (90–110 words)

---

### Useful Language for Argumentative Openings

| Function | Phrases |
|---|---|
| Introducing the topic | *In recent years… / It is widely acknowledged that… / Few topics have generated more debate than…* |
| Conceding a point | *While it is true that… / It cannot be denied that… / Although X plays a role…* |
| Stating the thesis | *This essay will argue that… / It is contended here that… / The following will demonstrate that…* |
| Signposting | *The discussion will first examine… / This essay proceeds as follows… / Three key areas will be explored…* |

## L6 — Speaking + Review

### Speaking Task: Structured Debate

**Motion:** *"To what extent does our cultural background define who we are?"*

---

**Format:**

| Stage | Time | Activity |
|---|---|---|
| **Preparation** | 2 minutes | Make notes (no script); choose your position |
| **Position statement** | 2 minutes each | Clearly state and support your view |
| **Open discussion** | 5 minutes | Challenge, concede and build on each other's points |
| **Summary** | 1 minute each | Summarise the key points of the debate |

---

**Language support:**

| Function | Expressions |
|---|---|
| **Giving opinions** | *I would argue that… / It seems to me that… / I'm firmly convinced that…* |
| **Adding emphasis** | *What strikes me most is… / The key point here is… / Above all,…* |
| **Conceding** | *While I accept that… / I take your point, however… / That may well be true, but…* |
| **Speculating** | *It's conceivable that… / One might suggest that… / I imagine that in many cases…* |
| **Challenging** | *I'm not entirely convinced by… / Could you explain what you mean by…? / Isn't it possible that…?* |
| **Building** | *To add to that point… / This is further supported by… / That links directly to…* |

---

**Discussion prompts (if needed):**
- Does your cultural background affect how you see yourself or how others see you?
- Can people ever fully escape the culture they were raised in?
- Is it possible to have a cultural identity that is entirely your own?
- How does migration affect a person's sense of cultural identity?
