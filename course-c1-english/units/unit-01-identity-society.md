### L3 — Grammar: Modal Verbs — Deduction and Probability

**Target structures:** *must, can't, could, might, may, should have + past participle*

**Form:**

| Degree of certainty | Present/Future | Past |
|---|---|---|
| Certain (positive) | *She must be tired.* | *She must have been tired.* |
| Certain (negative) | *He can't be serious.* | *He can't have said that.* |
| Possible | *It could/might/may be true.* | *It could/might have happened.* |
| Expected | *She should be here by now.* | *He should have arrived already.* |

**Use notes:**
- *Must* = strong logical deduction (not obligation in this context)
- *Can't* = strong negative deduction
- *Could/might/may* = possibility; *might* is slightly more tentative
- *Should have* = something expected but possibly unfulfilled

**Controlled practice:**
1. Rewrite 8 sentences using the appropriate modal for each context
2. Error correction — identify the modal errors in 5 sentences
3. Context-based: Read a short scenario and write 3 deductions using different modals

**Communicative practice:**
- Look at 4 photographs of different people in different situations. Make 3 deductions about each person using modals.

### L3 — Grammar: Perfect Aspect — Nuances

**Target structures:** Present perfect simple vs continuous · Perfect infinitive · Perfect aspect in reported speech

**Form and use:**

| Structure | Form | Use | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Present perfect simple | *have/has + past participle* | Completed action with present relevance | *She has lived in three countries.* |
| Present perfect continuous | *have/has been + -ing* | Ongoing or recently stopped action | *He has been studying Arabic for two years.* |
| Perfect infinitive | *to have + past participle* | Past reference after modal or main verb | *She seems to have left. He is believed to have known.* |
| Past perfect | *had + past participle* | Earlier past action (narrative or reported) | *By the time we arrived, they had already gone.* |

**Key contrasts:**
- *I've read the book* (completion, relevance now) vs *I've been reading the book* (process, ongoing)
- *He appears to have made a mistake* (past event viewed from present)

**Exercises:**

### L3 — Grammar: Advanced Reported Speech

**Target structures:** Complex transformations · Reporting verbs beyond *said/told*

**Reporting verb patterns:**

| Pattern | Verbs | Example |
|---|---|---|
| verb + that | *claim, argue, suggest, insist, admit, deny, agree, warn* | *She argued that social roles were socially constructed.* |
| verb + object + to-infinitive | *tell, encourage, advise, warn, urge, remind, persuade* | *He urged the committee to reconsider.* |
| verb + -ing | *deny, admit, suggest, recommend, justify, mention* | *She denied having known about the policy.* |
| verb + object + of | *accuse, convince, remind, assure* | *He accused her of misleading the public.* |
| verb + preposition + -ing | *apologise for, insist on, object to, agree to* | *They insisted on reviewing the data.* |

**Backshift rules** (advanced):
- Modal verbs: *will → would, can → could, may → might, must → had to / must*
- Time references: *now → then, today → that day, tomorrow → the following day*
- No backshift needed when: the situation is still true, or reporting is immediate

**Exercises:**
1. Transform 8 direct speech sentences using varied reporting verbs
2. Rewrite a short dialogue as a reported summary (100 words)
3. Error correction × 5 — reporting verb patterns and backshift mistakes

### L5 — Writing: Counter-argument and Refutation

**Writing focus:** Structuring a paragraph that presents and refutes an opposing view

**Analysis tasks:**
1. Identify: the opposing view, the challenge, the alternative position
2. What language signals the counter-argument? The refutation?
3. How does the writer avoid dismissing the other view outright?

### L3 — Grammar: Cleft Sentences

**Target structures:** *It is/was… that/who…* · *What + clause + is/was…*

**Form:**

| Type | Structure | Example |
|---|---|---|
| *It*-cleft | *It + be + focused element + that/who + rest* | *It was the social pressure that changed her mind.* |
| *Wh*-cleft | *What + subject + verb + be + focused element* | *What concerns me most is the lack of dialogue.* |
| Reverse *Wh*-cleft | *focused element + be + what + clause* | *The thing I find most troubling is what you said.* |

**Use notes:**
- Cleft sentences shift focus to a particular element of the sentence
- *It*-clefts are common in both spoken and written C1 English
- *Wh*-clefts are especially common in academic writing and formal speech

**Exercises:**
